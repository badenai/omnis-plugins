## The Iron Law

```text
Always calculate projected API/compute costs and validate ROI before recommending continuous, multi-agent autonomous workflows.
```

## Behavioral Rules

- When proposing autonomous or agentic workflows, always highlight the risk of runaway operational costs (e.g., rapid scaling to $1,000+/day in API fees).
- If ideating enterprise AI solutions, prioritize features that address the emerging AI FinOps market, such as cost optimization, compute monitoring, and strict budget limiters.
- When identifying high-volume or massive-context tasks (e.g., entire codebase analysis or long video processing), route requests to models utilizing aggressive pricing strategies like Google Gemini 1.5 Pro.
- If evaluating complex STEM or multi-step reasoning tasks, recommend OpenAI o1-mini to achieve an 80% cost reduction compared to heavier reasoning models.
- When assessing the replacement of human engineers with AI workflows, explicitly weigh the human labor savings against the "Cost-Productivity Paradox" of continuous agentic loops.
- Do not recommend "always-on" 24/7 agent architectures without defining explicit execution boundaries and cost caps.

## Red Flags

| Domain-Specific Rationalization | Why Wrong |
| :--- | :--- |
| "This autonomous agent architecture will automatically save money by replacing a human engineer." | Fails to account for steep, unmonitored daily API/compute fees and the unproven enterprise ROI of continuous agentic loops. |
| "Use the most powerful flagship model for all steps in the pipeline to ensure quality." | Ignores aggressive, specialized pricing alternatives (e.g., Gemini 1.5 Pro's cheap context window or o1-mini's cost efficiency). |
| "We don't need to worry about token limits since the context windows are now massive." | Conflates context capacity with financial viability; processing 2 million tokens per request rapidly drains operational budgets. |
