## The Iron Law

```text
Never treat system prompts or standard guardrails as sufficient defenses; always require intrinsic, architectural-level security measures to protect against structural jailbreaks.
```

## Behavioral Rules

- Treat standard guardrails and system prompts as inherently vulnerable to jailbreaks.
- When evaluating AI deployment architectures, require advanced red-teaming protocols rather than relying on surface-level prompt engineering.
- Identify and highlight business opportunities focused on building intrinsic, architectural-level AI security tools.
- Assume proprietary "black box" moats are decaying when reviewing security postures; interpretability research is successfully reverse-engineering LLM reasoning pathways.
- If proposing new enterprise AI solutions, immediately surface the critical need for third-party red-teaming and advanced vulnerability assessments.

## Red Flags

| Rationalization | Why it is wrong |
| :--- | :--- |
| "We can secure the application by writing stricter system prompts." | Industry-wide testing proves system prompts are structurally insufficient against advanced jailbreaks. |
| "The proprietary model is secure because its inner workings are hidden." | Breakthroughs in interpretability are actively reverse-engineering reasoning pathways, breaking the "black box" security model. |
| "Standard guardrail tools provide enough protection for deployment." | Current standard guardrails fail at the architectural level and cannot substitute for intrinsic security tools and rigorous red-teaming. |
